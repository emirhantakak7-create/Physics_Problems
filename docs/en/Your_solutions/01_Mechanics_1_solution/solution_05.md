# 5. Relative Velocity

A river flows east at v_r = 2 m/s.
A boat travels at v_b = 5 m/s in still water.
The river is d = 200 m wide.
The boat wants to travel directly north across the river.

We need to find the heading angle and the time it takes to cross.

---

## Step 1: Set up the velocity vectors

To travel exactly North, the boat must head somewhat West (upstream) to counteract the river's Eastward flow.
Let `theta` be the angle West of North.

The horizontal component of the boat's velocity must perfectly cancel the river's velocity:
v_b * sin(theta) = v_r
5 * sin(theta) = 2

---

## Step 2: Find the heading angle

Solving for theta:
sin(theta) = 2 / 5
sin(theta) = 0.4
theta = arcsin(0.4)

theta ≈ 23.58°

So, the boat must head approximately 23.58° West of North.

---

## Step 3: Find the resultant velocity across the river

The boat's effective speed directly across the river (North) is the vertical component of its velocity.
Using the Pythagorean theorem:
v_north^2 + v_east^2 = v_b^2
v_north^2 + 2^2 = 5^2
v_north^2 + 4 = 25
v_north^2 = 21

v_north = sqrt(21) ≈ 4.58 m/s

---

## Step 4: Calculate the crossing time

The time to cross the river depends only on the distance and the effective speed directly across:
t = d / v_north
t = 200 / sqrt(21)

t ≈ 43.64 seconds

---

## Final Result

Heading Angle: **23.58° West of North**
Time to cross: **43.64 seconds**
